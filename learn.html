Mexico's coffee regions

Mexico produces around 4 million bags of coffee a year, mostly from five southern states. Each region has its own altitude, microclimate, and character — and almost none of it reaches the international specialty market. Hover a region to learn more.

farms worth the trip

Nothing replaces standing in a coffee farm. The smell of the flowers, the weight of a ripe cherry, watching someone sort beans by hand — it reframes every cup you drink after that.

Guatemala

farm + museum

Finca La Azotea

Antigua, Guatemala — 1,500m

A working farm and coffee museum a few kilometres from Antigua's main square. Guided tours walk you through the full process — from the cherry on the tree to a finished espresso — with exhibits explaining each stage in plain language. The museum section covers the Mayan history of cacao and coffee in the region. This is the place that inspired the bar concept for Bean Town.

Best time to visit: November–January (harvest season). Book the morning tour — it includes the roasting demo.

watch & read

voices worth following

A short list. Not comprehensive — just the things we'd send someone who said "I want to actually understand coffee" without wanting a twelve-part masterclass.

YouTube — free

James Hoffmann

The best explainer in coffee. Hoffmann has the rare ability to be technically precise without being condescending. His videos on espresso, filter, and origin are the closest thing to a coffee education that doesn't require a barista certification. Start with his "What is Specialty Coffee?" video.

youtube.com/@jameshoffmann →

Book

The World Atlas of Coffee

Also by Hoffmann. Country by country, origin by origin — the physical object of understanding coffee as a global crop. Not a technical manual; more like a well-researched travel guide for the curious drinker. The photography alone makes it worth owning.

James Hoffmann, 2014 (updated 2018)

YouTube — free

World Barista Championship

Watch the competition rounds not to learn technique but to hear how the best baristas in the world talk about the farmers they work with. The presentations are essentially five-minute stories about a specific coffee and the people who grew it. That part's genuinely moving.

youtube.com/@WorldCoffeeEvents →
