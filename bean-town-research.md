*Last updated: June 2026*

## What consumers actually want to learn (and aren't getting)

### The primary gap: cultural coffee, not technical coffee

The specialty coffee world is dominated by western-centric, technique-first content. James Hoffmann (2M+ YouTube subscribers) owns the accessible-technical lane. What nobody is doing well:

- **Traditional preparations from producing countries** — how people in Ethiopia, Vietnam, Colombia, Mexico actually drink their coffee at home. Not as a specialty product to be evaluated, but as a daily cultural ritual.
- **The pastry and food pairing angle** — no significant creator links coffee culture to the food culture of its origin country systematically.
- **Non-English coffee cultures** — Japanese kissaten culture, Vietnamese iced coffee traditions, Turkish coffee ceremonies, Saudi qahwa — all massively underrepresented in English-language content.
- **Country-specific coffee history** — why did Vietnam become a robusta country? What does Indonesian coffee culture look like after centuries of Dutch colonial cultivation? The political and economic backstory to what's in the cup.

### What the data says consumers want

From 2024–2025 market research:

- **66% of 25–39 year olds in the US drank specialty coffee** in the past week (2024 National Coffee Data Trends) — the audience exists and is growing
- Core motivators: **curiosity, sensory experience, flavour, origin** — not brand or packaging
- Consumers increasingly demand **transparency and origin storytelling** — they want to know where it comes from and why it tastes that way
- The **at-home barista** is rising — people investing in equipment and education to replicate café experiences
- **Gen Z is shifting away from black filter coffee** toward more experiential and culturally distinct beverages — cold brew, espresso customisation, international preparations
- **Asia-Pacific coffee markets growing at 12.2% CAGR** (2025–2030) — China, Japan, Indonesia, Vietnam all expanding rapidly; almost no English-language content covers these cultures from the inside

### The anti-pretension gap

Per Perfect Daily Grind (2026): specialty coffee's greatest weakness is that it "reinforces the idea that the practices of importing nations in the Western Hemisphere are the default... casting other countries and their approaches as deviations from the norm."

Bean Town's positioning directly addresses this. The robusta used in Vietnamese cà phê đá isn't "lower quality" — it's the correct bean for that preparation. The cinnamon and piloncillo in café de olla aren't additives degrading a pure coffee experience — they *are* the coffee experience in that culture. This reframe is Bean Town's editorial spine.

### The content format gap

- Most coffee content is **video-first** — YouTube tutorials, TikTok brewing reels. Long-form written education about coffee culture almost doesn't exist outside of SCA trade publications.
- **Substack coffee** is almost empty — Boss Barista is the most notable, and it focuses on industry labour issues. A culturally-focused coffee newsletter has no clear competitor.
- **Coffee + travel** content is everywhere but surface level — "best cafés in Tokyo" listicles, not actual explanations of what Japanese kissaten culture is and why it matters.

**The white space:** Independent, English-language, culture-first, anti-pretension coffee education with global scope and a food pairing dimension. Nobody owns this.

## Content opportunity map

### High demand, low supply (priority topics)

1. **Vietnamese coffee culture** — cà phê trứng, cà phê sữa đá, the role of robusta, why condensed milk, street culture vs café culture. Adam has Japan access; Southeast Asia is adjacent.

2. **Mexican café de olla** — the clay pot preparation, piloncillo vs sugar, cinnamon as flavour not addition, how it's served at breakfast with pan dulce. Alex has direct lived context here.

3. **Ethiopian coffee ceremony** — jebena, three rounds, incense, social function. The origin country of coffee itself. Almost no accessible English content explains the ceremony from a cultural angle rather than a "look how exotic" angle.

4. **Japanese kissaten culture** — the post-war jazz café phenomenon, siphon brewing, the way coffee became a signifier of modernity. Adam is in Japan every winter.

5. **Turkish/Middle Eastern coffee** — qahwa, cardamom, fortune telling from grounds, café as male social space historically. The original coffeehouse culture.

6. **Colombian coffee at home vs for export** — Colombians famously drink tinto (weak, sweet, black), not the washed single-origin everyone exports. This disconnect is genuinely interesting.

7. **Northern Ireland/Irish coffee culture** — the builder's tea influence, the Barry's vs Bewley's debate, instant coffee's strange hold despite proximity to quality. Self-aware, humorous angle.

### Medium demand, interesting territory

- Indonesian kopi tubruk and kopi luwak (with honest take on the latter)
- Italian coffee rules (no cappuccino after 11am — why, and is it worth following?)
- Cuban café cubano and the colada tradition
- Yemeni qishr (coffee husk drink)
- Spanish café con leche vs cortado vs café solo — the regional variations

### Emerging / forward-looking

- **Asia-Pacific coffee boom** — China's specialty scene growing 12.4% annually; genuinely new coffee culture forming in cities like Shanghai, Chengdu
- **Decaf rehabilitation** — a Colombian decaf won the 2024 US Brewers Cup; the stigma is lifting
- **Functional coffee** — mushroom coffee trend, adaptogens; take a sceptical but open position
