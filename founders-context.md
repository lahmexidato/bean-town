## The Vulfpeck connection — why it matters for tone

Bean Town's name is directly inspired by Vulfpeck's *Dean Town* (the bass-only track from *Mr. Finish Line*, 2017). This isn't a casual reference — it's the **north star for brand energy:**

- Vulfpeck are world-class musicians who play with infectious joy and zero self-seriousness
- *Dean Town* is a technically extraordinary piece of music that sounds like a party
- They have a devoted cult following built on genuine craft + genuine personality, not marketing
- Their aesthetic: lo-fi in a deliberate way, warm, inclusive, a little odd, always authentic

Bean Town aspires to the same: **genuinely excellent coffee knowledge, delivered like you're at a party with someone who happens to know everything about it.** The moment it starts feeling like a lecture or a brand, it's lost the thread.

## Brand origin story (for About page / Substack intro)

*Draft — to be refined by Alex and Adam together*

Bean Town started in a conversation between two friends from Northern Ireland who between them have lived in Japan, Mexico, New Zealand, Spain, Edinburgh, and a few places in between.

We kept noticing the same thing: the way people drink coffee tells you something true about the place they're from. The clay pot and piloncillo in a Mexican kitchen. The three-round jebena ceremony in Ethiopia. The canned coffee from a vending machine outside a ski lodge in Hokkaido. None of it needed to be evaluated against a cupping score to be interesting.

So we made a brand to explore that. We're not baristas, we're not roasters, we're not World Barista Champions. We're just two people who travel, drink coffee, and think the interesting questions aren't about extraction ratios — they're about why this cup tastes like this, here, and what that means about the people who made it.

4th wave, we're calling it. Less about perfecting the craft, more about understanding the culture. First round's on us.

## Content voice — how Alex and Adam write differently

**Alex tends to:**
- Lead with the insight or the finding — editorial instinct from data journalism
- Frame things comparatively ("in Spain vs in Mexico, the same drink means different things")
- Find the structural angle — "here's why this is interesting, here's the pattern"
- Drier, more economical prose

**Adam tends to:**
- Lead with the experience — "I was at this kissaten in Furano and..."
- Sensory and personal — what it smelled like, who was there, what was on the walls
- More conversational, wandering slightly before landing
- Warmer on first read

**The combination:** Alex structures, Adam personalises. Neither writes like a coffee professional, which is the point. The voice is two friends with good instincts, not two experts with credentials.

## CREAM precedent — what Bean Town can learn

CREAM Snowboarding (Adam's existing brand) is the closest structural model:

| CREAM | Bean Town equivalent |
|---|---|
| Hand-drawn logo by founder | Hand-drawn logo by Adam |
| Near-black + off-white palette | Espresso + cream palette |
| Heavy custom type, underground feel | Olive/earthy palette, warm journal feel |
| Anti-corporate instruction | Anti-pretension education |
| Print-on-demand merch (t-shirts, stickers) | Stickers to start, t-shirts later |
| Furano camps as the physical product | Substack as the primary product |
| Password-protected site during build | Same approach — launch when ready |

Key difference: CREAM has a very specific niche (snowboard instruction in Japan). Bean Town is broader in scope — which means it needs a tighter editorial constraint to stay focused. The country edition format is that constraint.
