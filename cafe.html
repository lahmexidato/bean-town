the bean town café

it doesn't exist yet.

Here's the blueprint.

"Most specialty coffee shops are technically impressive and socially terrifying. We want to build the other kind."

Third wave coffee is extraordinary. The quality, the sourcing, the care that goes into getting a single-origin bean from a small farm to your cup — it's genuinely remarkable. But walk into most places that do it well and you'll feel it: the pressure to already know what you're ordering.

Bean Town is built on a different premise. The knowledge is the product. The experience of understanding something new about where your coffee came from — that's what you're there for. Not the extraction ratio. The story behind it.

how it works

01

you sit down.

No takeaway. No to-go cups. The experience can't be rushed and it doesn't need to be. If you're here, you're here. The cup stays in the room.

02

the bar tells a story.

Walk the length of the bar to order. On the wall behind the barista: the coffee's whole journey — cherry on the tree, the picking, the processing, the roast. By the time you reach the end, you know something you didn't at the start.

03

the drink fits the country.

We don't just serve single-origin from Ethiopia — we serve the jebena ceremony alongside roasted barley, the way it's done in Addis. Coffee as it was meant to be drunk, where it came from.

the bar

a walk through the whole thing

The bar runs the length of the room. You walk it left to right on the way to ordering. The wall beside you shows everything that happened before your cup existed.

  1. 1

    the farm

    A coffee tree takes 3–4 years to produce its first crop. A single tree yields around 2,000 cherries a year — roughly one pound of roasted coffee. That's one tree. One year.

  2. 2

    the picking

    Specialty coffee is almost always picked by hand, cherry by cherry, each one checked for ripeness. That's why it costs what it does. That's also why the person who picked it matters.

  3. 3

    the processing

    Washed. Natural. Honey. The method changes the flavour more than almost anything else. Washed is clean and bright. Natural is fruity and funky. Honey is somewhere in between. None of those words mean what they sound like, which is half the problem with how specialty coffee talks to people.

  4. 4

    the roast

    Temperature, time, colour. The roaster decides how much of the origin to preserve and how much to transform. Lighter roasts keep more of the terroir — the tasting notes you actually read on the bag. Darker roasts bring in the roast itself. Neither is better. They're different things.

  5. 5

    your cup

    You're at the end of the bar. You know what you're ordering. You sit down with it.

Inspired by Finca La Azotea in Antigua, Guatemala — a working farm that walks you through the whole process in real life, from the cherry on the tree to the cup in your hand. If you're ever in Antigua, go.

what's on

The menu doesn't change much. Each country gets its cultural drink and the thing that belongs beside it. Not the specialty interpretation — the original. The one that exists for reasons that have nothing to do with extraction ratios.

Mexico

Café de olla

+ a concha

Simmered with cinnamon and piloncillo in a clay pot. This is the home version — the one your abuela had a strong opinion about — not a specialty interpretation. Drunk slowly, before anything else happens in the morning.

Ethiopia

Jebena ceremony

+ roasted barley

Three rounds from the same grounds, each cup lighter than the last. The first for the host. The second for the guest. The third for a blessing. Coffee was born in Ethiopia and this is how it's still drunk there. You don't rush it.

Japan

Kissaten drip

+ wagashi

The kissaten is a serious coffee shop, not a café. Quiet, precise, unhurried. Japan took coffee, slowed it right down, and built a whole room around it. The pour-over is made in front of you like it matters. Because it does.

the space

shoes at the door

You take them off on the way in. It's a signal — you're entering a different kind of space, one worth slowing down for. The idea comes partly from the remove-your-shoes ritual of places built for presence. Once you're in, you're in.

beans like a market

Coffee is displayed by origin country the way fruit sits in a good greengrocer — bananas with bananas, apples with apples. Not shelved with scores and terminology you need a glossary for. Organised visually, navigably, like something you're meant to look at, pick up, and ask about.

nowhere to be

No wifi password. No working-from-your-laptop tables. The point isn't productivity — it's presence. You're there for the drink, the story behind it, and the person next to you who's there for the same reason.

First to know when it opens — and everything we're building in the meantime.
